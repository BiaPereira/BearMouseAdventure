If you've never seen the Dolomites at sunrise, add it to your list immediately. The jagged pink limestone towers are unlike anything else in the Alps — and they're even better at pace.

## The Route

We ran a condensed version of the **Alta Via 1**, a classic long-distance trail that stretches 120km from Lago di Braies to Belluno. We covered the northern half in 3 days:

| Day | Route | Distance | Elevation |
|-----|-------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Lago di Braies → Rifugio Biella | 22km | +1,800m |
| 2 | Rifugio Biella → Rifugio Lagazuoi | 18km | +1,400m |
| 3 | Lagazuoi → Cortina d'Ampezzo | 14km | -2,100m |

## Gear I Relied On

The terrain is technical in places — rocky, loose, with some via ferrata sections. Here's what made the difference:

- **Shoes**: Hoka Speedgoat 5 (incredible grip on wet limestone)
- **Pack**: Salomon ADV Skin 12 (enough for layers, food, emergency kit)
- **Poles**: Black Diamond Distance Carbon Z (foldable, lifesaving on descents)

## Refugios: The Hidden Gem

One of the great joys of running the Dolomites is the **rifugio system**. These mountain huts serve hot pasta, local wine, and apple strudel at altitudes above 2,000m. Don't skip them — they're worth every euro.

## Practical Info

- **Best season**: July to September
- **Getting there**: Fly to Venice or Innsbruck, then bus/train to Dobbiaco
- **Booking**: Refugios fill fast in August — book 2-3 months ahead
- **Permits**: None required for trail running
