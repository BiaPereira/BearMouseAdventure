The alarm went off at 2:00 AM. Outside the refuge, the temperature had dropped to -10°C. This was it.

## The Climb Begins

We had spent the previous day acclimatizing at the Goûter Hut (3,835m), watching other climbers make their way up and down the snow-covered ridge. Now it was our turn.

The headlamps carved yellow tunnels into the darkness as our small team of three set off across the glacier. Every step on the compacted snow felt deliberate, purposeful.

> "The mountains are calling and I must go." — John Muir

## The Final Push

By 6:00 AM we had reached the shoulder at 4,500m. The sun was just beginning to paint the horizon in shades of pink and amber — one of those moments you can't photograph well enough to do it justice.

The last 300 metres to the summit (4,808m) were the hardest. The air was thin, each step requiring conscious effort. But then —

## The Summit

At 7:43 AM, we stood on top of Western Europe.

The view stretched endlessly: the Matterhorn to the east, the Eiger to the north, and below us, a sea of clouds hiding the valleys. I'd been on a lot of peaks, but nothing quite like this.

## What I Learned

1. **Train specifically** — general fitness isn't enough; altitude training matters.
2. **Start early** — snow softens dangerously in afternoon sun.
3. **Respect the mountain** — three of the six climbers who set off with us turned back. That's okay.
